# Buyer Readiness Model

_(AI-Native Replacement for Traditional Sales Pipelines)_

## Why This Exists

Traditional CRM pipelines model **seller activity**, not **buyer reality**.

Stages like _Discovery → Demo → Proposal → Negotiation_ describe what reps do — not what buyers experience, decide, or risk. As a result:

- Forecasts are unreliable
- Data is low-quality
- Reps lie (or guess) to move deals forward
- Leaders confuse motion with progress

In an AI-native world, we no longer need humans to:

- Manually classify deals
- Populate dropdowns
- Guess buyer intent

Instead, we can model what actually matters:

> Buyer readiness across a set of load-bearing pillars — each measured continuously, not stage-gated.

This framework replaces linear pipeline stages with **parallel readiness pillars**, each expressed as a percentage of completeness and supported by evidence, context, and guidance.

---

## Core Design Principles

- Buyers do **not** move linearly
- Readiness develops **in parallel**
- Deals can close with **imbalances** (and those imbalances predict risk)
- AI observes reality; humans interpret and act
- Surface simplicity, drill-down depth

---

## The Four Information Types (Used Consistently Everywhere)

Each pillar is structured using the same four layers:

### 1. Signals

Evaluative indicators that drive readiness (% complete)

### 2. Anchors

Objective, observable facts that ground signals in reality

### 3. Context

Human explanation that reconciles signals and anchors

### 4. Guidance

Coaching, risk interpretation, and recommended interventions

---

## Cross-Pillar Lens (Not a Pillar)

### Trust & Credibility Lens

Applied across **all pillars**, never owned by one:

- Confidence vs hedging
- Trust in AI accuracy
- Trust in vendor partnership
- Verification-seeking behavior
- Risk flags and doubt signals

---

# The Buyer Readiness Pillars

## Pillar 1: **Problem Pressure**

**Definition:**

The degree to which the cost of inaction is high enough that a decision must be made.

This pillar represents **energy and urgency**, not solutions.

### Signals

- Pressure Intensity
- Time Sensitivity
- Consequence Clarity
- Leadership Visibility

### Anchors

- Catalyst events (missed forecast, lost deals, board pressure)
- Explicit leadership mandates
- Quantified impact (revenue, time, credibility)
- Operational burden indicators

### Context

**Ownership, causality, tolerance, and decisiveness**

- Who feels the pressure and how shared it is
- Why the pressure exists (their stated cause)
- Why it hasn’t been fixed already
- How (or if) pressure turns into a decision

### Guidance

- Coaching: quantify cost of inaction, tie to executive outcomes
- Risk: chronic pain, false urgency, single-threaded pressure
- Intervention: force consequence clarity, validate urgency with leadership

---

## Pillar 2: **Decision Framing**

**Definition:**

The degree to which the organization has clearly defined _what decision is being made_, _why_, _by when_, and _what success looks like_.

This pillar represents **mental structure**, not urgency or belief.

### Signals

- Decision Existence
- Decision Clarity
- Success Definition
- Alternative Awareness
- Decision Path Coherence

### Anchors

- Explicit decision statements
- Success criteria artifacts
- Named alternatives (including status quo)
- Decision timing anchors
- Evaluation or RFP artifacts

### Context

**Clarity, comparison, and resolution**

- What decision they think they are making
- How success is defined (and by whom)
- What alternatives are truly in play
- Why the decision hasn’t already been made

### Guidance

- Coaching: define decision in one sentence, separate learning from deciding
- Risk: exploration traps, solution-led framing, diffuse success
- Intervention: force decision definition, validate status quo as a choice

---

## Pillar 3: **Value Conviction**

**Definition:**

The degree to which the buyer believes this solution will solve the framed problem and deliver defensible ROI relative to alternatives.

This pillar represents **belief they can defend**, not interest.

### Signals

- Problem–Solution Fit Confidence
- Outcome Credibility
- ROI Defensibility
- Differentiation Clarity
- Price-to-Value Alignment

### Anchors

- Customer-articulated value statements
- ROI references and calculations
- Comparison behavior (Gong, Chorus, internal builds, etc.)
- Workflow-to-problem mappings
- Pricing reactions tied to value

### Context

**Belief, comparison, defense, and doubt**

- How they explain value in their own words
- How they compare alternatives
- How ROI is framed and defended
- Where skepticism or doubt remains

### Guidance

- Coaching: anchor value to their problem framing
- Risk: demo-led excitement, checklist comparisons, AI skepticism
- Intervention: force value articulation, defend ROI at exec level

---

## Pillar 4: **Stakeholder Alignment**

**Definition:**

The degree to which people with power, influence, and day-to-day impact are aligned and supportive.

This pillar represents **human alignment**, not process or budget.

### Signals

- Champion Strength
- Economic Buyer Alignment
- Technical / Operational Alignment
- End-User Buy-In
- Blocker Exposure

### Anchors

- Stakeholder participation in meetings
- Champion actions and internal selling
- Explicit buy-in statements
- Objections and resistance artifacts
- Evidence of internal communication

### Context

**Presence, priorities, sincerity, and resistance**

- Who is involved vs assumed
- What each stakeholder cares about
- Where alignment is strong vs superficial
- Where resistance or silence exists

### Guidance

- Coaching: map stakeholders explicitly, arm champions
- Risk: single-threaded champions, silent blockers
- Intervention: bring skeptics in early, validate economic buyer support

---

## Pillar 5: **Commercial & Procurement Readiness**

**Definition:**

The degree to which the organization can realistically complete the transaction without hidden friction.

This pillar represents **mechanics**, not desire.

### Signals

- Budget Reality
- Pricing Acceptance
- Authority Clarity
- Procurement Path Clarity
- Timeline Credibility

### Anchors

- Budget references and cycles
- Pricing discussions and discount behavior
- Named approvers
- Legal / security / procurement requirements
- Renewal or deadline anchors

### Context

**Money, perception, process, and friction**

- Where the money comes from
- How pricing is perceived
- How the buying process really works
- What could slow or derail the transaction

### Guidance

- Coaching: anchor price to value, clarify process early
- Risk: phantom budget, process blindness, timeline illusion
- Intervention: map procurement steps, validate authority

---

## Pillar 6: **Implementation Readiness**

**Definition:**

The degree to which the organization is operationally prepared to deploy successfully and achieve time-to-value.

This pillar represents **execution feasibility**.

### Signals

- Ownership Clarity
- Resource Availability
- Technical Fit Confidence
- Access Readiness
- Time-to-Value Confidence

### Anchors

- Named implementation owner
- Access commitments
- Tech stack artifacts
- Prior implementation history
- Start-date or timeline references

### Context

**Ownership, complexity, experience, and breakpoints**

- Who owns implementation and how real that ownership is
- What complexity actually exists
- What past experience shapes expectations
- Where execution could break

### Guidance

- Coaching: clarify ownership, set early win milestones
- Risk: ownerless implementations, hidden complexity
- Intervention: require owner naming, validate access early

---

## Pillar 7: **Organizational Adoption Readiness**

**Definition:**

The degree to which people will actually use the solution and achieve outcomes after go-live.

This pillar represents **behavioral reality**, not deployment.

### Signals

- Manager Buy-In
- User Sentiment
- Change Readiness
- Enablement Preparedness
- Success Definition Clarity

### Anchors

- Manager participation
- User exposure to the product
- Adoption history of past tools
- Training and rollout artifacts
- Defined success metrics

### Context

**Change climate, management stance, motivation, reinforcement**

- How change is perceived internally
- How managers view the solution
- What motivates or blocks users
- How success is reinforced

### Guidance

- Coaching: position as leverage, not surveillance
- Risk: shelfware, manager resistance, change fatigue
- Intervention: manager-first enablement, define 90-day wins

---

## What This Replaces

- Linear pipeline stages
- Manual field population
- Rep-guessed probabilities
- Lagging indicators
- False certainty

## What This Enables

- Continuous, AI-derived readiness
- Honest forecast confidence
- Early risk detection
- Better coaching
- Better buying experiences
- Better post-sale outcomes
