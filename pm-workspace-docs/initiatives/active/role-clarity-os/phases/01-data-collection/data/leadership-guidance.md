## PM Rubric Framework: The Four Buckets

Sam shared a rubric used at larger companies to evaluate PM performance and guide career progression. The framework divides PM skills into four core areas:

### 1. Execution

**Focus**: Requirements gathering, team cadence, quality delivery

**Key responsibilities:**
- Specifying clear requirements and acceptance criteria
- Maintaining team cadence and sprint delivery rhythm
- Ensuring quality standards are met
- Unblocking the engineering team
- Working collaboratively to drive outcomes (not just outputs)

**Expected behaviors:**
- Complete something deliverable within each 2-week sprint
- Own outcomes at sprint, milestone (multiple sprints), and launch (quarter) levels
- Think in terms of "we're a team trying to drive an outcome" not just individual tasks

**Current gaps identified:**
- Tyler taking on too much without asking for help when appropriate
- Role boundaries blurring (role collapse) - doing work that could be delegated
- Need to surface capacity constraints and set boundaries on what's achievable

### 2. Customer Insight

**Focus**: Understanding the user deeply

**Key responsibilities:**
- User research and discovery
- Analytics and data interpretation
- Usability testing (consistent, not ad-hoc)
- Understanding user needs and pain points

**Expected behaviors:**
- Talk to early majority users, not just innovators/early adopters
- Conduct proper usability testing - watch them use it, don't sell it
- Approach testing with "tell me why this won't work for you" (avoid bias)
- Test with less sophisticated, newer users (not just beta feature enthusiasts)

**Current gaps identified:**
- Usability testing not happening consistently before releases
- Talking primarily to innovators/early adopters (beta feature users)
- Beta/alpha releases used as usability tests instead of strategic pre-release testing
- Insufficient follow-up on PostHog data for feature flag users

### 3. Product Vision and Strategy

**Focus**: CEO-like thinking about product direction

**Key responsibilities:**
- Product strategy and roadmap
- Prioritization and saying no
- Long-term vision development
- Outcome-oriented thinking (not output-oriented)

**Expected behaviors:**
- Think about what outcomes will be driven, not just what's interesting to build
- Prioritize based on maximum ROI and impact, not just "good ideas"
- Balance current delivery (Horizon 1) with future innovation (Horizons 2-3)
- Focus on the "when" not just the "what" - timing matters for ROI

**Current gaps identified:**
- Gravitating toward "shiny, fun things" instead of most impactful work
- Spending time on low-value work (rubber balls) while dropping critical deliverables (glass balls)
- Context-switching across too many different types of work
- Overpromising timelines, missing deadlines, disappointing stakeholders

### 4. Influence and Communication

**Focus**: Stakeholder management, executive communication

**Key responsibilities:**
- Clear communication and alignment
- Executive-level communication framing
- Cross-functional coordination
- Decision presentation

**Expected behaviors:**
- Over-communicate rather than under-communicate
- Frame communications clearly: FYI vs input-needed
- Use DMs for priority items
- Present decisions in structured format: Background → Problem → Solution → Success Metrics → Impact
- Exercise executive communication framing skills

**Current gaps identified:**
- Sometimes leading with "what do you guys think?" instead of structured recommendation
- Expensive meetings (CEO, CRO, design head, eng head) without clear decision framework
- Need more practice with executive communication patterns

## PM Types by Company Stage

Sam explained that the type of PM skills needed varies dramatically by company lifecycle stage:

### Exploration Stage (Very Early)
**Characteristics:**
- Don't know what will stick yet
- Need to try many things and experiment
- High uncertainty, rapid iteration
- Focus on finding initial product-market fit

**PM Type Needed:**
- Comfortable with ambiguity
- Experimental mindset
- Willing to throw away work
- Founder-like operator

### Ramp-Up Stage (Hardest - Where AskElephant Is Now)
**Characteristics:**
- Have some product-market fit, entering growth
- Need to build the ship while it's flying
- Things changing fast, but can't afford complete chaos
- At cusp of crossing the chasm (innovators → early majority)

**PM Type Needed:**
- Combination of founder mindset AND structured execution
- Can deal with fast-moving chaos
- Balance experimentation with scaling
- **Hardest PM type to find and be successful as**

### Extract Stage (Later Scale)
**Characteristics:**
- Established product with large user base
- Optimizing for impact at scale
- High downside risk from mistakes
- Example: "Takes a year to move a pixel at Google, but that pixel is $50M impact"

**PM Type Needed:**
- Extremely outcome-oriented
- Process-driven, risk-aware
- Comfortable with bureaucracy and dependencies
- Patient with slow, high-impact changes

**Tyler's Context:**
AskElephant is in the Ramp-Up stage - the hardest phase. Tyler needs both experimental agility AND structured execution discipline. This explains why the role feels chaotic and why balancing priorities is so challenging.

## Product Strategy Frameworks

Sam referenced several foundational product strategy frameworks during the conversation. These provide mental models for understanding market positioning, innovation management, and product development lifecycle.

### 1. Crossing the Chasm

**Core Concept**: Technology adoption lifecycle and the critical gap between early adopters and mainstream market

**Key Elements:**
- **Innovators** (2.5%): Technology enthusiasts, willing to try anything new
- **Early Adopters** (13.5%): Visionaries who see strategic advantage
- **THE CHASM**: Critical transition point where most startups fail
- **Early Majority** (34%): Pragmatists who need proven solutions
- **Late Majority** (34%): Conservatives who adopt when forced
- **Laggards** (16%): Skeptics who resist change

**The Bowling Alley Strategy:**
- Hit the "lead pin" - establish a beachhead in one specific segment
- Expand to adjacent pins progressively (land and expand)
- Don't attack from 50 different fronts - you'll lose every beachhead
- Like a military campaign: establish first beachhead, then advance

**Why This Matters for AskElephant:**
- Currently at the chasm - moving from innovators to early majority
- Danger: Only talking to innovators/beta users won't reveal early majority needs
- May need to lose some custom features for innovators to appeal to mainstream
- Early majority users are pragmatists - they need proven, reliable solutions, not experiments

**Example Discussed:**
OnePlus phone manufacturer started with innovator-focused features (sandstone back, invite system) but had to abandon those brand-defining elements to cross the chasm and compete with Apple/Samsung in mainstream market.

**Application to AskElephant:**
- Need to talk to early majority users (less sophisticated, newer users)
- Can't rely on beta feature enthusiasts for product direction
- May need to simplify or change features that work for innovators
- Focus on reliability, ease-of-use, proven value (not cutting-edge experimentation)

### 2. Three Horizons Model

**Core Concept**: Managing multiple innovation timelines simultaneously to avoid innovator's dilemma

**The Horizons:**
- **Horizon 1**: Current business - optimize and defend
- **Horizon 2**: Emerging opportunities - scale new growth engines
- **Horizon 3**: Future possibilities - experiment and explore

**S-Curve Management:**
- Every product/business follows an S-curve (slow start → rapid growth → plateau/decline)
- Danger: When Horizon 1 plateaus, it's too late to start Horizon 2
- Must start Horizon 2 while Horizon 1 is still growing
- Must start Horizon 3 experiments while Horizon 2 is still early

**The Innovator's Dilemma:**
- At the plateau, it's STILL most profitable to focus on existing business
- Rational to keep investing in current success (high ROI)
- But if you don't start the next S-curve early, you'll be disrupted
- This is why successful companies fail - they're rationally focused on current business

**Resource Allocation:**
- While working on Horizon 1 (current delivery), must also invest in Horizon 2 (next growth)
- While working Horizon 2, must experiment with Horizon 3 (future)
- Need enough Horizon 3 experiments to ensure one achieves takeoff
- Compounding effects matter - new horizons should leverage existing assets

**Amazon Example:**
- Horizon 1: Online bookstore
- Horizon 2: Expanded to all e-commerce categories
- Horizon 3: AWS, Prime, marketplace, devices
- Each new horizon leveraged existing infrastructure/capabilities
- Started each horizon before previous one plateaued

**Application to AskElephant:**
- Don't just focus on current feature delivery (Horizon 1)
- Need parallel work streams for scaling features (Horizon 2) and future exploration (Horizon 3)
- This explains why Tyler has 9 different work streams - managing all three horizons
- Challenge: balancing all three horizons with limited PM capacity

1. Problem definition and research

### 4. Communication Frameworks

Sam emphasized multiple structured communication frameworks for different contexts. These are critical PM skills for effective influence and decision-making.

#### STAR Format (Interview/Story Format)
**When to use**: Telling stories about past work, interview responses, retrospectives

**Structure:**
- **S**ituation: Context and background
- **T**ask: What needed to be accomplished
- **A**ction: What you specifically did
- **R**esult: Outcome and impact

**Example**: "We had a 30% churn rate (Situation). I needed to identify and fix the root cause (Task). I conducted 20 user interviews and found onboarding confusion (Action). We redesigned onboarding and reduced churn to 15% (Result)."

#### SCQA Format (Sales/Stakeholder Format)
**When to use**: Sales conversations, presenting to stakeholders with different contexts

**Structure:**
- **S**ituation: Current state of the world
- **C**omplexity: What makes this difficult or nuanced
- **Q**uestion: The key question that needs answering
- **A**nswer: Proposed solution

**Tyler's note**: Sam mentioned this is specifically for sales but Tyler doesn't love it. The complexity element is useful for change management and dealing with political/organizational constraints.

#### Executive Communication Format (Product Decision Format)
**When to use**: Presenting product decisions to leadership, decision documents, project briefs

**Structure:**
1. **Background**: Context and current situation
2. **Problem**: Specific problem to solve (not just interesting work)
3. **Solution**: Recommended approach (with alternatives considered)
4. **Success Metrics**: How we'll measure success
5. **Impact**: Expected business/user outcome

**Tyler's Learning Experience:**
First meeting with Woody: Tyler came with just the problem, meeting went 90 minutes in circles, ended with "this was a huge waste of time, no proposed solution, let's redo tomorrow."

Second meeting: Used executive framework, presented Background → Problem → Solution → Metrics. Meeting took 5 minutes, got clear decision, everyone left aligned.

**Key Principle: Come with Recommendations**
- Never lead with "what do you think?" in expensive meetings (CEO, CRO, design/eng heads)
- Always come with "here's my recommendation" based on analysis
- Present options if truly uncertain, but include your recommended choice
- Respect that executive meetings are expensive - make decisions, don't just brainstorm

#### Running Doc for Action Items
**When to use**: Ongoing 1-on-1s and project tracking

**Structure:**
- Maintain running document of action items and decisions
- Review at each meeting to ensure nothing drops
- Track commitments and follow-ups
- Prevents "starting fresh every time" syndrome

**Sam's Challenge:**
Sam noted he gets meeting notes automatically transcribed but doesn't actually review them or follow up on action items. This leads to dropping commitments and starting fresh each conversation instead of building on previous decisions.

**Opportunity:**
Tyler and Sam should establish a shared running doc format to track:
- Action items from each 1-on-1
- Decisions made and their rationale
- Topics to cover in future meetings
- Progress on long-running initiatives
